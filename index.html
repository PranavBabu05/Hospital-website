Pheumonia

Pneumonia is an infection that inflames the air sacs in one or both lungs, which may fill with fluid or pus. It can be caused by bacteria, viruses, or fungi and often leads to cough, fever, and difficulty breathing. Prompt medical treatment is essential, especially for young children, the elderly, or those with weakened immune systems.

Diabetes

Diabetes Mellitus is a chronic disease that causes high blood sugar due to lack of insulin or the body’s inability to use it properly. It can lead to symptoms like frequent urination, thirst, and fatigue. Long-term complications include heart disease, kidney damage, and nerve issues.

Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)

Hypertension, or high blood pressure, is a condition where the force of blood against artery walls is consistently too high. It often has no symptoms but can lead to serious problems like heart disease, stroke, and kidney damage. Regular monitoring and lifestyle changes are key to managing it.

Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD)

Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) is a progressive lung disease that makes it hard to breathe, often caused by long-term exposure to irritants like cigarette smoke. Common symptoms include coughing, wheezing, and shortness of breath. It cannot be cured but can be managed with medication, oxygen therapy, and lifestyle changes.

Congestive Heart Failure (CHF)

Congestive Heart Failure (CHF) is a condition where the heart cannot pump blood effectively to meet the body’s needs. This leads to fluid buildup in the lungs, legs, and other parts of the body. Symptoms include shortness of breath, fatigue, and swelling, and it requires ongoing medical management.

Stroke (Cerebrovascular Accident)

A stroke, or cerebrovascular accident (CVA), occurs when blood flow to part of the brain is interrupted or reduced, depriving brain tissue of oxygen.

DEPARTMENTS

Pneumonia

In our pneumonia is typically managed by the Pulmonology or Respiratory Medicine department. Severe cases may also require care from the Intensive Care Unit (ICU) if respiratory support is needed. Collaboration with Infectious Diseases may occur for complex or resistant infections.

Diabetes

In the hospital, diabetes is treated by monitoring blood sugar levels closely and administering insulin or oral medications as needed. Patients receive tailored meals and fluids to help stabilize glucose levels.

Hypertension

In the hospital, hypertension is treated by monitoring blood pressure and administering antihypertensive medications. Severe cases may require intravenous drugs and close observation in a critical care setting.

Chronic

Chronic diseases, such as diabetes, hypertension, or COPD, are long-term conditions requiring ongoing management. In the hospital, treatment focuses on stabilizing symptoms, adjusting medications, and preventing complications.

Congestive

Congestive heart failure (CHF) is a condition where the heart can't pump blood effectively, leading to fluid buildup. In the hospital, treatment includes oxygen therapy, diuretics to remove excess fluid, and medications to improve heart function.

Stroke

A stroke occurs when blood flow to the brain is blocked (ischemic) or a blood vessel bursts (hemorrhagic), causing brain damage. In the hospital,the type—clot-busting drugs or thrombectomy for ischemic stroke, and surgery or blood pressure control for hemorrhagic stroke.
